All polar color spaces displayed here (HSL, HWB, LCH and OKLCH) use "shorter hue", the default hue interpolation method. The other three possible values are not listed here as they have specific use cases and are likely not what you require for general styling. For more info, visit this link .

  1. TL;DR
    1. Define your color stops using a standard color function (RGB, HEX, HSL or HWB).
    2. For each specific gradient, test both OKLAB and OKLCH for interpolation space, compare the results and pick the one you prefer.

    background: linear-gradient( <direction>  in  <interpolation-space> ,  <color-stop-fn-1> ,  <color-stop-fn-2> );
    background: linear-gradient( to right  in  oklab ,  hsl(. . .) ,  hsl(. . .) );

  2. Default values
    1. Colors expressed in hexadecimal notation belong to the RGB color space; the HEX format is merely an alternative syntax for RGB color function, representing the same RGB values.
    2. If no interpolation space is specified, gradients whose color stops are defined exclusively with the RGB, HSL or HWB functions default to the sRGB interpolation space. For all other cases, the gradient defaults to the OKLAB interpolation space. (Chrome)
  3. Interpolation spaces
    1. The interpolation space has a significantly greater impact on the resulting color gradient quality than the color stop function. Specifically:
      • A chosen interpolation space will generally yield consistent results across various color stop functions.
      • Conversely, a specific color function will produce markedly different results depending on the interpolation space selected.
      Therefore, selecting the appropriate interpolation color space is the primary factor in achieving the desired color gradient fidelity.
    2. OKLAB and OKLCH are the only viable color interpolation spaces. All others are deemed too inconsistent across variations in hue, lightness, and saturation, as well as between different browsers, making them unsuitable for reliable color work. On the other hand, while OKLAB and OKLCH spaces offer the most reliable quality, they each present specific limitations; therefore, direct comparison between the two is essential to determine the optimal visual outcome.
    3. Using any polar color space like HSL, HWB or LCH as the interpolation color space is strongly discouraged. These spaces frequently produce inconsistent mid-tone results when combined with any color function, and they often introduce unintended colors into gradients. The only polar color space that works consistently is OKLCH.
    4. If one of the color stops is a pure white color, use OKLAB for interpolation, as in those conditions OKLCH in Chrome, Opera, Edge and Safari adds extra colors to mid-tones.
    5. If a gradient has neighboring color stops that differ by 60 or more degrees in hue, OKLCH will in most situations preform better than OKLAB, as OKLAB adds grayish mid-tones.
  4. Color functions
    1. Viable color functions for defining color stops are:
      • In OKLAB interpolation: RGB, HEX, HSL, HWB, OKLAB, OKLCH
      • In OKLCH interpolation: RGB, HEX, HSL, HWB
    2. All others are too incosistent across browsers
  5. Additional notes
    1. Firefox can not properly render conic gradients. Colors are fine, but the gradient has too sharp hue steps.
    2. Absurdly, Chrome, Opera and Edge can not properly render gradients which use the same OKLCH for both interpolation space and color function.
    3. If one of the color stops is a pure white color, Chrome, Opera, Edge and Safari do not interpolate properly in polar color spaces. Interpolation produces different gradients through polar and rectangular color spaces and across browsers in general. OKLCH is the preferred interpolation color space and while it generally yields superior results, in blue-white gradients for example, blue mid-tones can acquire a subtle teal hue. The alternative is OKLAB, which tends to introduce a faint purple tint in blue mid-tones. Similar hue shifts can be observed on other white-to-saturated gradients. Choose the option that best aligns with your visual requirements.

    4. Gradients spanning large hue differences (like a transition from light pink to blue) vary significantly based on whether rectangular (e.g., Oklab) or polar (e.g., Oklch) color interpolation is used.
      • Rectangular Interpolation (Oklab) Rectangular color spaces interpolate along a straight line across the color wheel. This path often passes near the achromatic center, causing a significant loss of saturation (a "graying out" or muddying) in the mid-tones of the gradient, especially with complementary colors. In the pink-to-blue example, the blue component tends to dominate the final gradient appearance.
      • Polar Interpolation (Oklch) Polar color spaces interpolate in a circular manner around the hue wheel. This method effectively maintains saturation, preventing the desaturation observed in rectangular methods. However, this approach introduces additional hues (like pink, magenta, and purple in the example) into the mid-tones, which can result in a more complex color trajectory.
      • Both interpolation methods are considered viable, and the choice between them ultimately depends on the specific design requirements of the gradient.
